The Seljuk Turks

Prior to the era of the Crusades, the Levant was under the control of the Seljuk Turks. According to the course textbook, "Turkish pastoral nomads from Asia had been in contact with Islam from the early period of the Arab conquests, and Turks had served as professional soldiers in the armies of various Abbasid caliphs" (Cleveland 33). The Seljuk Turks had an empire which ranged much of the Middle East, including the Levant. There was one other Muslim rival in the region, the Fatimid Empire in Egypt. This competition meant the Seljuks Turks could not call upon the muslims of egypt for support, in fact they had to fight against them. Ultimatly this fragmentation left the Seljuk Turks weak to attack. "Following the Seljuks’ defeat of the Byzantine army at the battle of Manzikert in 1071, these migrating tribes moved into Anatolia and began the gradual transformation of that land from a Greek-speaking Christian territory to a Turkish-speaking Muslim one" (Cleveland 33). Another direct effect of the battle of Manzikert 1071 was the Byzantine call upon the pope for aid against the Seljuk Turks. This call for aid is what would start the movement for the first crusade to begin.

Religious Freedoms

The Seljuk Turks were officially a Muslim ruling class. Specifically, "The Seljuks are traditionally characterised by their avid support for Sunnism" (Peacock 249). The Turks took on the role as defenders of the faith of Islam and delved deep into their practice and upholding of the Islamic faith. According to the course textbook, "the Seljuks were responsible for a rejuvenation of Sunni Islam; it was the Seljuk minister Nizam al-Mulk who founded the madrasah system of state-sponsored education for the ulama" (Cleveland 33). Much like other Muslim Rulers preceding them, "Seljuk sultans did not seek to enforce religious conformity" (Peacock 285). Instead, the Turks had Jews or Christians pax extra taxes or to wear certain colors of clothes to show what religion they follow. Furthermore, "The Seljuk employment of senior Shi‘ite and Jewish officials" or "the alliance of various sultans with the Ismailis" (Peacock 285) give the impression of a more practical approach when interacting with other religions within the empire. While the Jewish and Christian classes were still clearly defined as second class citizens, they did have the opportunity to live in relative peace without an overarching pressure by their rulers to convert to the state religion to avoid persecution.

Trade Routes

Before the Crusades there were three main trade routes from the Mediterranean to the entire east of the globe. The first was the Silk Road, up the Persian Gulf, and up the Red Sea. "Until the Crusades, the only access had by Western merchants to the wares carried along these routes was the great emporium of Constantinople" (Day 159-160). In general, this means that trade from the Levant to Europe was primarily routed and handled through Constantinople. Being that the Byzantine Empire and the Sejluk Turks did not get along would have been a strain on the Levant's ability to trade with Europe. Through a quick analysis of a map of the Levant, one may realize that there is a lot of shoreline for ports in the Meddeterranian to conncet the Levant Europe. While some ports and trade already existed, the trade network between the Levant and Europe was expanded upon during the Crusades. This is discussed further on this page. Trade between the Levant and the rest Middle East also was already long-established and would continue to be throughout the duration and after the crusades. The reason the Levants connection to Europe is the focus here is because of the major economic gains that were availble if it were to become another connection from the west to the east like the silk road.
