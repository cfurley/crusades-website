"It was into this politically fragmented Middle Eastern world of the eleventh and twelfth centuries that the European Crusaders made their first appearance and established the four Latin kingdoms of Edessa, Antioch, Tripoli, and Jerusalem" (Cleveland 34).

The Four Crusader States

The Crusader States included the Kingdom of Edessa, Kingdom of Antioch, Kingdom of Tripoli, and the Kingdom of Jerusalem. These states were run initially by Frankish nobles and were established following the Siege of Jerusalem in 1099. The Crusader States also housed the official Military Orders of the Crusades such as the Knights Templar and the Knights Hospitallar. During the Crusades, the Crusader States occupied the region of the Levant. They build giant castles such as Krak de Chevaliers and the castle at Acre. For the people of the Levant, they had been under Muslim rule for a long time. Now they were in an occupied, essentially holy war zone, that the Crusaders were able to impose their will upon for around 200 years. As we will see in the next section on the religous hierarchies set in place, the Crusader States put themselves above those of the land they ruled. By the middle to end of the Crusades, the Crusader States became more understanding and cooperative with the local populations of the Levant region in which they occupied. The population still felt the heavy pressence of the Crusader States and likely wished for more tolerant rulers to occupy the region of the Levant.

Jihad

Jihad is an Islamic Holy War, usually called to defend the faith. Muslim military forces gather and wage war against the enemies or attackers of the Islamic Faith. During the Crusades, much of the Islamic world very much had an enemy in the Crusader States whom occupied the Levant. In particular, a general by the name of "Saladin was confronted by the crusader states wedged between the two parts of his dominions" (Brand 167). Saladin also hated the Crusaders for religious reasons and believed that they had made desecrated the Temple Mount with shoes and pigs. Saladin declared Jihad against the Crusaders and waged war against them. Saladin and his Jihad forces would eventually take back Jerusalem, where he would famously pray to god to cleanse the holy grounds which the Crusaders had messed up. "In the later decade of Saladin's life, the counter crusade accelerated rapidly, with Saladin escalating his jihad and triumphing against the Crusaders" (Hamblin 103). Jihad has been a prevalent term used in Muslim Wars ever since. Prior to the Crusades, Jihad was not really a set in stone term. This terminology helps shape the culture of the Muslim population of the Levant. Jihad is something which is relatively normal to be called, especially in the modern era.
